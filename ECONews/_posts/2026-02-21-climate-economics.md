**Original Article:** [Climate Policies Reshape Economic Landscapes](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2026/feb/21/climate-policies-reshape-economic-landscapes)

## News Summary

Governments worldwide are implementing increasingly ambitious climate policies that are fundamentally reshaping economic activities. The article examines carbon pricing mechanisms, renewable energy subsidies, and regulatory changes affecting industries from energy to transportation to agriculture. These policies are creating both challenges and opportunities: traditional fossil fuel industries face transition costs, while clean technology sectors experience rapid growth. The analysis highlights distributional concerns, as the costs and benefits of climate policies are not evenly distributed across regions or income groups. Economists debate the most efficient policy mix to achieve climate goals while minimizing economic disruption and addressing equity concerns.

## IB Economics Analysis

This article connects to multiple units in the IB Economics syllabus, particularly **Unit 2: Microeconomics** and **Unit 4: The Global Economy**.

### Relevant Theories and Frameworks:

1. **Externalities (Unit 2.5):** Climate change represents a classic case of negative externalities. Students can analyze:
   - How greenhouse gas emissions create external costs not reflected in market prices
   - The difference between private costs and social costs
   - Diagrammatic analysis of externalities using marginal social cost/benefit curves

2. **Government Intervention (Unit 2.4):** The article provides examples of different policy approaches:
   - **Market-based solutions:** Carbon taxes and cap-and-trade systems (Pigouvian taxes)
   - **Command-and-control:** Regulations and standards
   - **Subsidies:** Support for renewable energy and clean technology

3. **Sustainable Development (Unit 4.7):** Climate policies directly relate to sustainable development goals. Students can analyze:
   - The tension between economic growth and environmental sustainability
   - Intergenerational equity considerations
   - The role of technology in achieving sustainable development

4. **Market Failure (Unit 2.5):** Beyond externalities, climate change involves:
   - **Common pool resources:** The atmosphere as a global commons
   - **Public goods:** Climate stability as a non-excludable, non-rival good
   - **Asymmetric information:** Challenges in measuring and verifying emissions

5. **Income Distribution (Unit 2.6):** The distributional impacts of climate policies raise important equity questions that students can analyze using economic concepts.

### Evaluation Points for IB Assessments:

1. **Policy Effectiveness:** Students could evaluate the relative effectiveness of different climate policy instruments (carbon taxes vs. regulations vs. subsidies).

2. **Efficiency vs. Equity Trade-offs:** The article provides material for evaluating the tension between economically efficient policies and their distributional impacts.

3. **Global Cooperation Challenges:** Students could evaluate why international cooperation on climate change has been difficult, connecting to concepts of free rider problems and prisoner's dilemmas.

4. **Innovation and Dynamic Efficiency:** An evaluation could consider how different policies affect incentives for technological innovation in clean energy.

### Connections to IB Economics Internal Assessment (IA):

This article could support an IA exploring:
- Cost-benefit analysis of a specific climate policy
- Comparative analysis of carbon pricing mechanisms in different countries
- The impact of climate policies on specific industries or regions
- Evaluation of renewable energy subsidies using economic criteria
