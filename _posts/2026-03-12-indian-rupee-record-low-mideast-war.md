**Original Article:** [Rupee tumbles to record low on Mideast war, central bank likely steps in](https://www.reuters.com/world/india/indian-rupee-hits-record-low-mideast-war-rattles-markets-stokes-economic-risks-2026-03-04/)

## News Summary

The Indian rupee weakened past 92 per US dollar for the first time on March 4, 2026, hitting a record low as an escalating Middle East war rattled global markets. The currency fell to 92.3025 against the dollar, down approximately 0.9% and surpassing its previous record low of 91.9875 reached in January 2026. This decline makes the rupee Asia's worst-performing currency in 2026, with a more than 2% depreciation since the start of the year. The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) is likely intervening to curb further losses, according to traders. The widening Middle East conflict poses significant risks for India's economy by potentially fanning inflation, hurting growth, and worsening the current account balance. India imports over 80% of its crude oil needs and relies heavily on the Middle East for remittances from its diaspora. Heightened risk aversion is also driving foreign investors out of Indian equities and bonds, with the benchmark Nifty 50 equity index falling nearly 2% and 10-year bond yields rising. The conflict has overshadowed optimism around a potential U.S.-India interim trade deal that market participants had hoped would support the rupee through lower tariffs and boosted exports.

## IB Economics Connections

This article provides a real-world case study connecting to multiple IB Economics syllabus topics. First, it illustrates **Unit 4.5: Exchange rate** dynamics, showing how geopolitical events (Middle East conflict) cause currency depreciation through increased risk aversion and capital flight. Students can analyze the demand and supply factors in the foreign exchange market, where heightened uncertainty reduces demand for emerging market currencies like the rupee while increasing demand for safe-haven assets like the US dollar. Second, the article relates to **Unit 4.6: Balance of payments**, as India's heavy dependence on oil imports (over 80% of needs) means higher oil prices worsen the current account deficit, while reduced remittances from the Middle East diaspora and foreign investment outflows affect the financial account. Third, it connects to **Unit 3.3: Macroeconomic objectives** - the rupee depreciation risks importing inflation through more expensive oil (cost-push inflation) while potentially hurting economic growth through reduced investment and consumption. Finally, the RBI's likely intervention demonstrates **Unit 3.5: Demand-management: monetary policy**, where central banks use foreign exchange reserves to stabilize currencies, though such interventions involve trade-offs between exchange rate stability and reserve depletion. Students can evaluate the effectiveness of different policy responses, including interest rate adjustments versus direct currency intervention.
