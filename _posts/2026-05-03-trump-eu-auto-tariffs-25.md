**Original Article:** [Trump says he will hike tariffs on EU cars to 25%](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c4g8zpylzz9o)

## News Summary

On May 1, 2026, U.S. President Donald Trump announced that he will increase tariffs on cars and trucks imported from the European Union to 25%, sharply escalating trade tensions between the two economies. Trump claimed the EU was not complying with the Turnberry trade agreement signed in July 2025, which had set a 15% tariff ceiling on most European goods in exchange for European investment in the U.S. and commitments to boost American exports. However, Trump did not provide specific details on how the EU had violated the agreement. The auto tariffs fall under Section 232 of the Trade Expansion Act of 1962, which permits duties on national security grounds, making them unaffected by the Supreme Court's February 2026 ruling that struck down Trump's broader "Liberation Day" tariffs imposed under different legal authority. In response, the European Commission stated it would "keep our options open to protect EU interests," while Bernd Lange, chair of the European Parliament trade committee, called the move "unacceptable," accusing the U.S. of repeatedly breaching the agreement. Trump urged European automakers — particularly Mercedes, BMW, and Volkswagen, which import a significant share of their U.S. sales from European plants — to shift production to the U.S., warning that producing domestically would mean "NO TARIFF."

## IB Economics Connections

This article provides an excellent real-world case study for IB Economics Unit 4.2: Types of trade protection, specifically tariffs. A tariff is a tax on imported goods that raises domestic prices, reduces import quantities, generates government revenue, and creates welfare loss. Students can apply the standard tariff diagram to analyze how a 25% import tax on EU cars affects the U.S. market: consumer surplus decreases as prices rise, domestic producers gain surplus, government collects tariff revenue, and deadweight loss emerges from allocative inefficiency. The article also connects to Unit 4.4: Economic integration, as the Turnberry Agreement represents a bilateral trade arrangement that reduced tariffs through mutual concessions — illustrating how trade agreements can be fragile and vulnerable to political tension. The national security justification (Section 232) relates to Unit 4.3: Arguments for and against trade protection, where protecting strategic industries is a common pro-tariff argument, while higher consumer prices and potential EU retaliation represent standard counterarguments. Students should evaluate this case by considering whether tariffs can effectively achieve their stated goals of protecting domestic industries, or whether they primarily create inefficiencies and retaliatory cycles that reduce the overall gains from trade.
