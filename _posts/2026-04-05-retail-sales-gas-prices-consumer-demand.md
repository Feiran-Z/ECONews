**Original Article:** [US retail sales strong in February; rising gasoline prices will hurt spending](https://www.reuters.com/business/us-retail-sales-increase-solidly-february-2026-04-01/)

## News Summary

U.S. retail sales increased by 0.6% in February 2026, showing stronger-than-expected consumer spending despite economic uncertainties. However, economists warn that rising gasoline prices following the Iran-Israel conflict will likely constrain future consumer spending. The February sales data, released by the Commerce Department, revealed broad-based gains across multiple sectors including motor vehicles, building materials, and online retailers. Core retail sales, which exclude automobiles, gasoline, building materials, and food services, rose by 0.3% in February. The solid retail performance occurred before gasoline prices surged to $4 per gallon in March due to Middle East tensions. Economists predict that higher fuel costs will force consumers to reallocate their spending, reducing disposable income available for other goods and services. This comes amid already elevated inflation, with the Federal Reserve's preferred inflation measure (PCE price index) rising 0.3% in January. The combination of strong February spending and impending gasoline price pressures creates a complex picture of consumer demand, where short-term resilience may give way to longer-term constraints as households adjust their budgets.

## IB Economics Connections

This article provides an excellent real-world case study of Unit 2.1: Demand in the IB Economics syllabus, specifically focusing on non-price determinants of demand. The rising gasoline prices demonstrate how prices of complementary goods affect demand for other products—as fuel costs increase, consumers have less disposable income for non-essential purchases, causing a leftward shift in demand curves for various retail goods. The article also illustrates the importance of consumer expectations: anticipation of future price increases (for gasoline and general inflation) can cause consumers to alter current spending patterns, either by accelerating purchases before prices rise or by cutting back in anticipation of tighter budgets. This connects to the concept of forward-looking behavior in demand theory. Additionally, the data shows how income distribution affects demand patterns—lower-income households are disproportionately affected by gasoline price increases, potentially creating divergent demand trends across different market segments. The February retail sales figures themselves demonstrate the law of demand in action, with quantity demanded responding to various economic conditions. This case study helps students understand the distinction between movements along a demand curve (price changes for specific goods) versus shifts of the entire demand curve (changes in non-price determinants like income, expectations, and prices of related goods). The geopolitical context (Middle East conflict affecting oil prices) further shows how external shocks can transmit through global markets to influence domestic consumer behavior.
