## News Summary

The U.S. Federal Reserve maintained its benchmark interest rate at 5.25-5.50% during its March 2026 policy meeting, marking the seventh consecutive meeting without a rate change. The decision comes amid persistent inflation concerns, with the latest Consumer Price Index (CPI) showing annual inflation at 3.2%, still above the Fed's 2% target. Fed Chair Jerome Powell emphasized that while progress has been made in reducing inflation from its 2024 peak of 9.1%, the central bank needs "greater confidence" that inflation is moving sustainably toward the 2% target before considering rate cuts. The Fed's updated economic projections indicate that most policymakers expect only one or two rate cuts in 2026, down from previous expectations of three cuts. The central bank also revised its inflation forecast upward, projecting core PCE inflation to end 2026 at 2.6%, slightly higher than December's projection of 2.4%. The decision reflects the Fed's cautious approach to balancing inflation control with economic growth, as the U.S. economy continues to show resilience with unemployment at 4.1% and GDP growth projected at 2.1% for 2026.

## IB Economics Connections

This Federal Reserve decision directly connects to IB Economics Unit 3.5: Demand-management: monetary policy. The Fed's interest rate decision represents contractionary monetary policy aimed at reducing aggregate demand to combat inflation. According to the money market model, maintaining high interest rates reduces the money supply's growth rate, increasing borrowing costs for consumers and businesses. This decreases consumption and investment spending, shifting aggregate demand leftward and reducing inflationary pressures. The Fed's inflation targeting framework (2% target) exemplifies how central banks use clear policy objectives to anchor inflation expectations, a key concept in Unit 3.5. The transmission mechanism operates through several channels: higher interest rates increase savings returns (encouraging saving over spending), raise mortgage and loan costs (reducing housing and durable goods purchases), and strengthen the dollar (reducing export competitiveness and import prices). The Fed's cautious approach highlights the evaluation of monetary policy limitations, including time lags (policy changes take 12-18 months to fully impact the economy) and the risk of overtightening causing recession. This case study demonstrates the trade-offs central banks face between inflation control and economic growth, a core theme in macroeconomic policy evaluation. Students can analyze this using AD-AS models to show how contractionary monetary policy reduces inflationary gaps but may create output gaps if applied too aggressively.
