**Original Article:** [London council tax rise planned to pay for police, mayor says](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/clyxqp539y7o)

## News Summary

London Mayor Sadiq Khan has proposed increasing council tax by approximately 4% for Band D properties to fund policing and crime prevention initiatives across the capital. The Greater London Authority (GLA) precept, which constitutes about a quarter of total council tax bills and primarily funds the Metropolitan Police, would rise by £20.13 from £490.38 to £510.51 annually for average households. This increase aims to generate over £100 million in additional funding compared to the 2025-26 budget, targeting specific crime-fighting priorities including phone theft prevention, support for victims of child sexual exploitation, violence against women and girls initiatives, and youth gang diversion programs. The Metropolitan Police has previously warned of significant funding gaps due to government cuts, necessitating difficult decisions about service reductions. The final budget requires approval from the London Assembly in the coming weeks, with the mayor emphasizing that tackling crime remains his top priority and that the tax increase represents a necessary investment in public safety.

## IB Economics Connections

This news article provides a practical example of public goods provision and the challenges of funding them, connecting directly to **Unit 2: Microeconomics**, specifically **Section 2.9: Market failure: public goods**. Policing represents a classic public good exhibiting both non-excludability (once provided, all residents benefit from reduced crime regardless of tax payment) and non-rivalry (one person's benefit from police protection doesn't diminish protection available to others). The free rider problem inherent in public goods explains why voluntary contributions would be insufficient - individuals would have incentive to avoid paying while still benefiting from the service, leading to underprovision. The council tax increase represents government intervention to solve this market failure through compulsory taxation, ensuring adequate funding for this essential service. This connects to **Section 2.7: Role of government in microeconomics**, where governments intervene to provide goods and services that markets fail to supply efficiently. Students can analyze the equity considerations of using a regressive tax (council tax) to fund public goods, examining trade-offs between efficiency and equity. The article also illustrates the political economy dimension of public goods provision, where elected officials must balance public safety needs with taxpayer burden, demonstrating real-world applications of economic theory in policy decision-making.
