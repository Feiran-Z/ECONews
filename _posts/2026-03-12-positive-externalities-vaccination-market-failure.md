**Original Article:** [NHS 'clearly failing' to ensure children get measles vaccine, experts warn](http://www.theguardian.com/science/2026/feb/16/nhs-children-mmr-vaccine-measles-vaccination-reform-pharmacies)

## News Summary

The UK's National Health Service (NHS) is facing criticism for failing to ensure adequate measles vaccination rates among children, with experts warning that the system needs urgent reform. In some parts of England, fewer than 60% of five-year-olds have received both recommended doses of the MMR (measles, mumps, and rubella) vaccine, far below the World Health Organization's recommended 95% threshold for herd immunity. In Enfield, north London, where a measles outbreak has affected 60 children (15 requiring hospitalization), the vaccination rate stands at just 64.3% - lower than Malawi's rate of 69.3% and only slightly above Afghanistan's 62%. Public health experts warn that more outbreaks are inevitable given these low vaccination rates. The situation has prompted calls for major reforms to England's vaccination system, including delivering jabs through pharmacies to improve accessibility. Measles can cause serious complications including brain damage, pneumonia, blindness, and even death, making vaccination a critical public health priority.

## IB Economics Connections

This article provides a clear real-world example of market failure due to positive externalities, connecting directly to IB Economics Unit 2.8: Market failure: externalities and common-pool resources. Vaccinations create significant positive externalities through herd immunity - when a sufficient proportion of the population is vaccinated, it protects even those who are not vaccinated by reducing disease transmission. In a free market, individuals may underconsume vaccinations because they consider only their private benefits (personal protection) and costs, ignoring the social benefits (reduced disease spread, lower healthcare costs, increased productivity). This leads to allocative inefficiency where the market equilibrium quantity (Q1) is less than the socially optimal quantity (Q2). Students can analyze this using marginal social benefit (MSB) and marginal social cost (MSC) diagrams, showing how MSB exceeds marginal private benefit (MPB) for vaccinations. The article demonstrates appropriate government interventions for positive externalities: direct provision through the NHS, subsidies (free vaccinations), educational campaigns, and potential regulatory solutions. This case also connects to Unit 2.9 (Market failure: public goods) as herd immunity exhibits characteristics of a public good - it's non-excludable (everyone benefits regardless of vaccination status) and non-rivalrous (one person's protection doesn't reduce protection for others). Students should evaluate the effectiveness of different policy approaches using criteria from the syllabus: efficiency in achieving herd immunity, equity in access across different socioeconomic groups, administrative feasibility of pharmacy-based delivery, and sustainability of funding for vaccination programs.
