**Original Article:** [Exclusive: Bostic says Fed cannot offset possible rise in structural unemployment](https://www.reuters.com/business/bostic-says-fed-cannot-offset-possible-rise-structural-unemployment-2026-02-24/)

## News Summary

In a recent interview with Reuters, Atlanta Federal Reserve President Raphael Bostic warned that the United States may be entering a period of structurally higher unemployment as companies increasingly deploy artificial intelligence (AI) tools to reduce labor costs. Bostic, who is stepping down from his position at the end of February 2026, stated that this technological transformation could fundamentally change what the Federal Reserve considers "full employment" for its dual mandate of price stability and maximum employment. He emphasized that traditional monetary policy tools like interest rate cuts may not be effective in addressing this type of structural unemployment, which results from fundamental changes in the economy rather than cyclical downturns. Bostic's comments highlight a growing concern among policymakers that AI-driven automation could lead to persistent unemployment as displaced workers struggle to find new jobs that match their skills, potentially requiring a reevaluation of how central banks approach labor market objectives.

## IB Economics Analysis

This article provides a compelling real-world example of **structural unemployment**, a key concept in the IB Economics syllabus under **Unit 3.3: Macroeconomic Objectives**. Structural unemployment occurs when there is a mismatch between workers' skills and the requirements of available jobs, often due to technological change, globalization, or shifts in consumer demand. Unlike cyclical unemployment (caused by economic downturns) or frictional unemployment (temporary job transitions), structural unemployment represents a more persistent problem that cannot be solved through traditional demand-side policies alone.

The article connects to several important IB Economics concepts:

1. **Types of Unemployment (Unit 3.3)**: The distinction between structural, cyclical, frictional, and seasonal unemployment is fundamental to macroeconomic analysis. Bostic's warning about AI-driven job displacement illustrates how technological change can create structural unemployment by making certain skills obsolete faster than workers can retrain.

2. **Natural Rate of Unemployment (Unit 3.3)**: The natural rate (or non-accelerating inflation rate of unemployment - NAIRU) represents the level of unemployment consistent with stable inflation. Bostic suggests that AI adoption may raise this natural rate, meaning the economy could sustain higher unemployment without triggering deflationary pressures.

3. **Limitations of Monetary Policy (Unit 3.5)**: The article demonstrates a key limitation of demand-management policies. While expansionary monetary policy (interest rate cuts) can address cyclical unemployment by stimulating aggregate demand, it is largely ineffective against structural unemployment, which requires supply-side solutions like education, training, and labor market reforms.

4. **Supply-Side Policies (Unit 3.7)**: This news highlights the importance of supply-side policies in addressing structural unemployment. Interventionist policies like investment in human capital (retraining programs) and market-based policies like labor market reforms become crucial when technological change creates skill mismatches.

5. **Economic Growth and Development (Unit 4.7-4.10)**: The AI-driven transformation represents both an opportunity for economic growth through productivity gains and a challenge for equitable development. Students can analyze the trade-offs between technological progress, employment stability, and income inequality.

For IB assessments, students could evaluate: the effectiveness of different policy responses to structural unemployment; the distributional impacts of technological change on different worker groups; and the trade-offs between encouraging innovation and protecting employment. This article also provides excellent material for Internal Assessments exploring the impact of automation on specific industries or demographic groups.
