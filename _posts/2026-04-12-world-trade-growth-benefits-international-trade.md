## News Summary

The World Trade Organization (WTO) reports that global merchandise trade growth is forecast to slow to 1.9% in 2026, down from 4.6% in 2025, with potential further deceleration if Middle East conflicts continue to disrupt energy markets and global transport. Last year's stronger-than-expected trade performance was driven by a surge in artificial intelligence-related goods and "front-loading" of shipments to avoid anticipated U.S. tariffs. Trade in AI-enabling goods accounted for 42% of global trade growth in 2025, despite representing only one-sixth of total trade. The WTO warns that sustained high energy prices from geopolitical tensions could reduce global merchandise growth by 0.5 percentage points, while services trade faces a 0.7-point drop from forecasted growth rates. Asia is projected to lead merchandise import growth in 2026 with 3.3% growth, followed by Africa at 3.2%. The report highlights how global trade remains resilient despite challenges, buoyed by continued demand for AI-related products and adaptive trade patterns.

## IB Economics Connections

This article provides a real-world illustration of Unit 4.1: Benefits of international trade from the IB Economics syllabus. The data demonstrates how international trade drives economic growth, with global merchandise trade expanding by 4.6% in 2025. The surge in AI-related trade exemplifies the concept of **comparative advantage** - countries specializing in high-tech manufacturing where they have relative efficiency gains. The 42% contribution of AI goods to trade growth shows how **specialization** based on technological capabilities creates significant economic benefits. The article also illustrates the **gains from trade** through increased variety and access to advanced technologies worldwide. However, the forecast slowdown to 1.9% growth in 2026 reveals the **vulnerabilities of interdependence** - geopolitical conflicts can disrupt trade flows and reduce benefits. The "front-loading" of shipments to avoid tariffs connects to trade protection topics (4.2), showing how policy changes affect trade patterns. This case study helps students understand both the static benefits (efficiency gains from current specialization) and dynamic benefits (technology transfer, innovation diffusion) of international trade, while also recognizing the risks from geopolitical instability that can diminish these gains.
