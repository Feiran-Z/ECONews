**Original Article:** [US budget deficit to keep growing amid Trump tax cuts, tariffs, CBO forecasts show](https://www.reuters.com/business/us-budget-deficit-grow-1853-trillion-fy2026-cbo-says-2026-02-11/)

## News Summary

The Congressional Budget Office (CBO) forecasts that the US budget deficit will grow to $1.853 trillion in fiscal year 2026, representing approximately 5.8% of GDP. This marks a slight increase from the $1.775 trillion deficit in fiscal 2025. The CBO projects that the deficit-to-GDP ratio will average 6.1% over the next decade, reaching 6.7% by fiscal 2036, significantly above the Treasury Secretary's goal of reducing it to around 3% of economic output. The report indicates that President Trump's economic policies, including tax cuts and tariffs, are worsening the country's fiscal position despite low economic growth projections. The CBO forecasts 2026 real GDP growth at 2.2%, fading to an average of 1.8% for the remainder of the decade, which contrasts with the Trump administration's more optimistic projections of 3-4% growth. Key factors driving the deficit include the extension of 2017 tax cuts through the "One Big Beautiful Bill" Act, which will boost consumer spending and private investment but also increase government debt. The CBO notes that sustained large deficits are historically unusual given projected unemployment rates below 5% throughout the budget window.

This article provides a real-world case study of fiscal policy and its macroeconomic implications, directly connecting to IB Economics Unit 3.6: Demand-management: fiscal policy. The US budget deficit situation illustrates expansionary fiscal policy through tax cuts and government spending, which increases aggregate demand (AD) in the short run but creates long-term sustainability concerns. Students can analyze this using the AD-AS model (Unit 3.2), showing how expansionary fiscal policy shifts AD to the right, potentially increasing real GDP but also risking inflationary pressures. The deficit-to-GDP ratio (5.8% in 2026) relates to Unit 3.3's objective of maintaining a sustainable level of debt, where high debt-to-GDP ratios can crowd out private investment and increase interest payments. The conflicting growth projections between the CBO (2.2%) and the Trump administration (3-4%) highlight the challenges of economic forecasting and policy evaluation discussed throughout the IB syllabus. Students should evaluate the effectiveness of this fiscal policy using criteria from Unit 3.6: time lags in implementation, targetedness of tax cuts, multiplier effects on consumption and investment, potential crowding out of private sector activity, and the non-reversibility of tax cuts once implemented. The article also touches on Unit 3.5 (monetary policy) implications, as large deficits may influence Federal Reserve decisions on interest rates to control inflation.
