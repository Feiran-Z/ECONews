## News Summary

Global government bond markets experienced a sharp selloff in early March 2026 as escalating Middle East tensions fueled inflation concerns and altered expectations for central bank interest rate cuts. Bond prices tumbled and yields surged across major economies, with two-year yields in Britain rising to 3.732% (from 3.516%), German yields climbing to 2.177% (reaching a one-year high), and U.S. Treasury yields jumping to 3.498%. The selloff was triggered by fears that prolonged conflict involving Iran would significantly boost energy prices, with Brent crude oil reaching $85.12 per barrel and European wholesale gas prices soaring over 40%. This market movement reflects investor concerns that higher energy costs will sustain inflationary pressures, complicating central banks' efforts to reduce interest rates. Traders dramatically scaled back expectations for monetary easing, reducing the perceived probability of a Bank of England rate cut from 75% to just 30%, while pushing anticipated Federal Reserve rate cuts from July to September. European Central Bank Chief Economist Philip Lane warned that a prolonged war could substantially spike euro zone inflation while reducing economic growth, with ECB analysis suggesting such an oil price shock could lift inflation by 0.5 percentage points.

## IB Economics Connections

This article provides a real-world illustration of how financial markets respond to changing inflation expectations and monetary policy outlooks, connecting directly to **Section 3.5: Demand-management: monetary policy** in the IB Economics syllabus. The bond market selloff demonstrates the inverse relationship between bond prices and yields, with rising yields reflecting increased inflation expectations that reduce the present value of future fixed payments. The shifting rate cut expectations exemplify how markets continuously reassess central bank policy paths based on evolving economic conditions, particularly inflation risks. The article also relates to **Section 3.3: Macroeconomic objectives**, specifically the challenge of maintaining low and stable inflation amid external supply shocks. The oil price surge represents a classic cost-push inflation scenario that can be analyzed using the **Section 3.2: Aggregate demand and aggregate supply model**, where supply shocks shift the short-run aggregate supply curve leftward, increasing price levels while potentially reducing output. For IB Economics students, this case study offers opportunities to analyze the transmission mechanisms between geopolitical events, commodity prices, inflation expectations, financial markets, and monetary policy decisions. The divergent market reactions across regions (stronger U.S. position versus European vulnerability) also illustrate how economic structures influence vulnerability to external shocks, connecting to **Section 4: The Global Economy**. Students can evaluate the effectiveness of monetary policy in addressing supply-side inflation versus demand-pull inflation, considering the trade-offs between inflation control and economic growth objectives.
